Introduction to Linear Programming

Linear Programming (LP) is a mathematical optimization technique for maximizing or minimizing a linear objective function subject to a set of linear constraints. It‚Äôs a fundamental tool used in business, economics, operations research, and engineering to find the best possible solution under given limitations.

Core Elements:

  • Objective Function: Represents the goal ‚Äî e.g., maximizing profit or minimizing cost.
  • Constraints: The limits or requirements that must be satisfied.
  • Decision Variables: Quantities you want to determine for optimization.
  • Non-negativity Restriction: Decision variables must be zero or positive.

Geometric Interpretation

The beauty of Linear Programming lies in its geometric visualization. Each constraint defines a half-plane, and their intersection forms a feasible region ‚Äî the set of all possible solutions that satisfy every condition.

1. Feasible Region

The feasible region is the shaded intersection of all constraint lines. It represents all valid solutions to the LP problem and is always a convex polygon in two dimensions.

2. Objective Function

The objective function:

represents a family of parallel lines. As we move these lines in the direction of increasing or decreasing Z, the line that just touches the feasible region gives the optimal solution.

3. Corner-Point Principle

The optimal solution always occurs at one of the vertices (corner points) of the feasible region. This is because linear functions achieve their maxima or minima at the boundaries of convex regions.

Key Takeaways

  • Feasible regions are always convex.
  • Optimal solutions occur at corner points.
  • Geometric visualization aids intuition and problem solving.
  • Every LP problem can be solved graphically (for 2 variables) or via the Simplex method (for higher dimensions).
